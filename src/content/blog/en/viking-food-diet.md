What did Vikings actually eat when they weren't raiding or trading? The honest answer is a lot less cinematic than the sagas suggest: porridge, flatbread, salted fish, soured milk. There was no spectacle here, only hard seasonal scarcity, met with a level of ingenuity that's easy to underrate. If you want to understand how the Norse really lived day to day, their food is a better starting point than their weapons — and it's here that archaeology quietly demolishes the Hollywood image of nightly feasts with whole roasted boars.

Diet in the North was, first and foremost, a matter of geography. Short summers, long winters, and precious little arable land squeezed between fjords and rock left almost no room for waste. Nearly everything that ended up on the table had to be wrung from thin soil, hauled out of the sea, or hauled in over long trade routes. That scarcity is exactly what pushed the Norse toward the preservation and fermentation techniques that still echo through Scandinavian and Icelandic kitchens today.

## Grain, Porridge, and the Price of Worn-Down Teeth

Grain formed the backbone of the diet — chiefly **barley**, along with rye and oats, while wheat remained something of a luxury reserved for wealthier households. Archaeobotanical finds from settlements like **Birka** in Sweden and **Hedeby** on today's German-Danish border have turned up charred grain in quantities substantial enough that historians now treat barley as the North's true staple crop. Most of it went into **porridge**, cooked with milk or water and occasionally sweetened with honey — a plain, filling dish eaten across every social rank, from thralls to chieftains.

Bread was baked too, usually as flat, unleavened cakes cooked on hot stones or in simple ovens. And this is where bioarchaeology delivers one of its most vivid — and least appetizing — findings. Skeletal remains from the Viking Age consistently show heavy, uniform **dental wear** far beyond what diet alone would explain. The culprit was the milling process itself. Grain was ground into flour using hand-operated **quernstones** made of sandstone or granite, and fine grit from the stone inevitably worked its way into the flour. Every loaf of bread, in other words, came with a pinch of literal grit, and a lifetime of eating it ground people's teeth down year after year. It's the kind of detail no saga bothered to record, yet it tells us more about daily Viking life than most heroic poetry ever could.

## Fish, Meat, and What Ended Up on the Plate

For most coastal communities, fish mattered more than meat, and for good reason — the sea was simply more dependable than any field. Herring, cod, salmon, and eel were caught and sometimes eaten fresh, but far more often **dried, salted, or smoked** to see people through the winter. Dried cod — an early ancestor of what Norway still sells today as *klippfisk* — could keep for months and was light enough to carry on long voyages. That kind of durable provisioning was precisely what made the epic distances covered by the [Viking longship](/en/blog/the-longship) possible in the first place: no crew was crossing the North Atlantic or running a weeks-long raiding campaign without dried fish and cured meat stowed aboard.

On land, **pork, mutton, and goat** dominated the farmyard — hardy, low-maintenance animals suited to poor grazing. Cattle were more valuable and tended to be kept for milk, leather, and labor rather than the cooking pot. One meat deserves special mention: **horse**. For wealthier households, horsemeat wasn't just food — it carried real **ritual weight** before Christianization took hold. Horse sacrifice and communal consumption of horsemeat were bound up with pagan cult practice, closely tied to the sacrificial feasts held in honor of the [Norse gods](/en/blog/norse-gods). As Christianity spread through the 10th and 11th centuries, eating horsemeat increasingly fell out of favor — church sources explicitly condemned it as a pagan custom, which, if anything, confirms just how ritually charged the practice had been.

Game, poultry, and eggs rounded things out, alongside vegetables like cabbage, onions, and peas, plus whatever wild berries the season and region allowed.

## Skyr, Soured Milk, and the Art of Not Spoiling

Dairy was everywhere in daily life, but fresh milk spoiled fast, and fermentation was the fix. Iceland gives us the best-known survivor of this approach: **skyr**, a thick, yogurt-like dairy product made by acidifying milk and draining off the whey. Icelandic sources and the later saga literature mention skyr so casually that it clearly wasn't a delicacy — it was just what people ate.

Equally telling is the Icelandic preservation method known as **"sur"**: food of almost every kind — meat, fish, whey, even ram's testicles — was submerged in slightly acidic whey, where it could keep for months or even years. This wasn't some minor curiosity; it was one of the central strategies that let Icelandic settlers survive winters where both livestock fodder and fresh human food ran perilously short.

> Picture Viking cuisine as an endless string of heroic feasts, and you miss how much daily life actually revolved around fermenting, drying, and salting — techniques that make for duller stories but were what actually kept people alive.

Excavations of the latrine and midden layers at **Coppergate in Jorvik** (Viking-Age York) provide unusually rich evidence here. Analysis of fecal deposits and kitchen waste reveals a surprisingly broad range of plants, grains, fish, and meat in the urban diet — and confirms that city dwellers ate far more varied fare than long assumed, including imported spices such as coriander and dill.

## Mead, Ale, and What People Actually Drank

Eating went hand in hand with drinking, and two beverages dominated: **ale**, brewed mostly from barley, and **mead**, fermented from honey. Both stretched well beyond simple nutrition — they were central to sacrificial feasts, funeral rites, and the highly formalized drinking rituals that structured social life. Honey, meanwhile, was by far the era's most important sweetener, since sugar in the modern sense simply didn't exist yet.

Wine, by contrast, remained an import, arriving in the North via trade routes from Frankish and Mediterranean lands and reserved almost exclusively for the elite — a status symbol, not a household staple. Traces of wine found in the graves of high-status individuals and at major trading hubs like Birka underline exactly that: it sat alongside imported silk and silver as a marker of prestige, not an everyday pleasure.

## What Archaeology Proves — and What's Still Guesswork

It's worth being precise about where the evidence actually stands. Archaeobotany and archaeozoology — the study of plant and animal remains recovered from settlement layers — provide solid evidence for the staples described here, for fermentation practices, and for the outsized role fish and grain played in the diet. The heavy dental wear caused by quernstone grit is likewise well documented, appearing consistently across multiple skeletal collections.

What tends to get exaggerated in popular accounts are exact recipes, precise quantities, or the image of nightly feasting on mountains of meat. No source records that level of detail, and such claims owe more to modern Viking marketing than to the archaeological record. The reality was more modest and more repetitive — and arguably more impressive for it: a culture that managed to systematically preserve food across one of Europe's harshest landscapes, provision long sea voyages, and still maintain trade networks reaching as far as the Byzantine Empire deserves at least as much credit for its kitchens as for its axes.

## How Often, and How, People Ate

The structure of Viking meals looked quite different from modern habits too. Most sources and archaeological contexts point toward **two main meals** a day — one in the mid-morning, once the first round of livestock chores was done, and one in the evening after darkness fell and farm work stopped. People generally ate together in the hall, seated on low benches, eating from shared bowls or plain wooden platters. Individual place settings in the modern sense barely existed: everyone carried their own knife, wooden or horn spoons were common, but forks were entirely absent across the whole Norse world.

Cookware from the period — iron cauldrons, soapstone pots, wooden bowls — turns up in both graves and settlement layers, and shows that most cooking happened over open fire or in pit ovens, where heated stones served as a kind of thermal battery. This technique of boiling water by dropping in glowing-hot stones is well attested archaeologically through so-called **cooking pits**, found at sites across Scandinavia, and appears to have been a standard method for heating large quantities of meat or water at once — useful for communal feasts in particular.

## Trade Routes, Spices, and the Finer Tables of the Elite

However modest the average farming household's diet was, finds from trading hubs like Birka, Hedeby, and Viking-Age York make clear that Norse cooking was hardly cut off from the wider world. The same trade routes that carried silver, amber, and enslaved people also brought imported **spices** north — coriander, dill, mustard seed, and occasionally even pepper, likely funneled through Byzantine or Arab intermediaries along the Volga trade route, which Norse merchants followed as far as the Caliphate.

Dried fruit, nuts, and in rare cases even figs have turned up in Viking-Age deposits — clear evidence that at least the upper tiers of society had access to a surprisingly cosmopolitan table that went well beyond barley porridge and dried fish. Finds like these complicate the picture of an isolated culture living purely off local resources, and point instead to a sprawling trade network that carried culinary variety all the way into the far North.

## Looking Back at the Hearth

Buy Icelandic skyr off a supermarket shelf today, or order Norwegian klippfisk at a restaurant, and you're eating a genuine continuation of Viking-Age preservation methods — not just a romanticized nod to them. That's rare in history: an everyday practice that has survived essentially unchanged for a thousand years simply because it worked.

Viking diet wasn't a footnote to raiding and saga-telling. It was the unglamorous foundation that let people survive, travel, and expand under some of the toughest conditions in Europe. Maybe that's the more interesting story after all — not the axe, but the cooking pot that made the whole Viking Age possible.
