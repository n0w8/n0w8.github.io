In an unassuming churchyard in the Danish town of Jelling stand two stones that together say more about the transition from the Viking Age into Christian medieval Europe than plenty of history books manage. The **Jelling Stones** aren't ordinary runestones of the kind found by the thousand across Scandinavia — they're royal monuments in which family love, political power, and religious upheaval are packed into an extraordinarily small space. Since 1994 they've stood, together with the two burial mounds and the church at Jelling, on the UNESCO World Heritage list, and for good reason: no other monument captures the moment Denmark became a nation and a Christian kingdom quite so compactly.

## 1. Two Stones, One Royal Line: Gorm and Harald Bluetooth

The two stones were raised by two successive kings of the same dynasty. The smaller, older stone was erected around the middle of the 10th century by **King Gorm the Old**, the first king of Denmark who's reasonably well attested historically. The larger, far more elaborate stone was raised by his son **Harald Bluetooth** (Harald Blåtand), who ruled around 965 AD and is generally credited with finally uniting Denmark and converting it to Christianity. Both stones originally stood between Jelling's two enormous burial mounds and were later moved closer to the site once the first church was built there.

Gorm himself is only dimly attested historically; it's really with his son Harald that Denmark enters a period of comparatively well-documented events. That father and son chose to raise their monuments at the very same spot, between two enormous, artificially raised burial mounds that predated the runestones themselves, was hardly a coincidence: Jelling was apparently already a significant assembly and cult site long before the Danish royal dynasty made it its symbolic center, and both rulers deliberately used this existing site to tie their own authority to an older, already established sense of the place's importance.

## 2. The Smaller Stone: Gorm's Declaration of Love for Thyra

Gorm's stone carries a comparatively short but moving inscription in Younger Futhark runes. Roughly translated, it reads: "King Gorm made this monument in memory of Thyra, his wife, Denmark's adornment." The wording is remarkable for several reasons. For one, it's among the earliest written appearances of the name **Danmörk**, Denmark, anywhere, making the stone essentially one of the oldest "birth certificates" of the Danish name. For another, dedicating a monument to a wife is unusually personal for a ruler's monument of this period, which some scholars read as a hint that Thyra herself may have held real political influence, possibly even hailing from a notable Anglo-Saxon or Frankish family.

Later saga traditions, which admittedly need to be read with real caution, embellish Thyra's role even further, portraying her as a shrewd advisor at her husband's side on matters of state. Whether such details are historically reliable or were added after the fact is hard to settle conclusively, but the simple fact that a 10th-century Danish king dedicated such an elaborate, publicly visible monument to his wife at all is remarkable for the period, and clearly sets the smaller Jelling stone apart from the runestones elsewhere in Scandinavia, most of which focus on men and their feats of arms.

## 3. The Larger Stone: Harald's Claim to Power in Runes and Images

The great Jelling stone, often called "Denmark's birth certificate," carries one of the most famous runic inscriptions of the entire Viking Age:

> "King Harald ordered this monument made in memory of Gorm, his father, and in memory of Thyra, his mother — that Harald who won for himself all of Denmark and Norway, and made the Danes Christian."

This inscription is far more than a dedication to his parents. It's one of the earliest surviving pieces of political self-presentation by a Scandinavian ruler, a text that deliberately ties dynastic legitimacy, territorial claim, and religious change together in a single sentence. In it, Harald claims not only control over Denmark but Norway as well, a claim whose actual historical extent historians still debate today, particularly since rule over Norway at the time was probably loose and heavily dependent on local alliances rather than any continuous administration.

The stone's sheer physical scale is striking too: at roughly 2.4 meters tall with inscriptions carved across three faces, it ranks among the largest and most elaborate runestones in all of Scandinavia, a scale almost certainly chosen deliberately so that no visitor to the site could miss Harald's claim to power. The great Jelling stone has aptly been described as something like a king's stone press release, issued by a ruler who had to hold his realm together precisely because its unity was anything but a given.

## 4. Christ in a Serpent's Coil: The Jelling Style and Denmark's Conversion

What makes the larger stone even more distinctive are its two large carved images. On one side it bears what is probably the **oldest surviving depiction of Christ in Scandinavia**, a figure with outstretched arms entwined in ribbon-like ornamentation that looks more like Celtic knotwork than later Christian iconography. On the opposite side, a large winged beast coils around a serpent, a motif that gave an entire artistic style of the period its name: the **Jelling style**, one of the most influential strands of Viking-era ornament.

Pairing a beast-and-serpent motif with a depiction of Christ on the very same stone is no accident. It visualizes exactly what the inscription states in words: the shift from a world shaped by animal symbolism and the old gods toward a new Christian order, with Harald evidently placing both visual languages side by side quite deliberately, to anchor his authority with both his still-pagan subjects and his newly converted ones.

## 5. The Burial Mounds: What Archaeology Found Beneath Jelling

Jelling's two massive burial mounds, North and South, rank among the largest of their kind in Denmark. Excavations in the north mound, in the 1820s and again in the 1970s, uncovered an empty but originally richly furnished burial chamber, dendrochronologically dated to 958 or 959 — a date many archaeologists connect to Gorm's burial. Curiously, the chamber was already empty at the time of excavation, giving rise to the theory that Harald Bluetooth, after his own conversion to Christianity, had his father's pagan remains exhumed and reburied in consecrated ground beside the newly built church, an act that may have been driven equally by piety and political calculation. Bone finds beneath the present-day Jelling church, which indeed match a robustly built middle-aged man, support this theory without proving it conclusively.

The south mound, meanwhile, despite being even larger than the north one, turned out on excavation to contain no burial evidence at all, a puzzle that stumped archaeologists for a long time. The now-favored explanation is that Harald had this mound built not as an actual grave but purely as a symbolic monument, an enormous earthwork meant to underscore his power and the site's significance without ever serving a burial function. Dendrochronological analysis of timber remains from both mounds dates the construction work precisely to Harald's reign, further confirming just how deliberately he shaped the entire site as a unified statement of power, burial mounds, runestones, and eventually the first church all included.

## 6. From Runestone to Bluetooth Logo: An Unexpected Legacy

Few Viking-age monuments have had an afterlife quite as unexpected as the great Jelling stone. When Swedish and Finnish engineers developed a short-range wireless technology in the late 1990s meant to connect devices from different manufacturers, they named it after **Harald Bluetooth**, as a symbol of the technology's ambition to unite different "tribes" of the electronics industry the way Harald once united Denmark. Even the globally recognized **Bluetooth logo** is a direct link: it combines the Younger Futhark runes for Harald's initials, ᚼ (H) and ᛒ (B), into a single bindrune. So anyone pairing a phone with wireless headphones today is, without realizing it, using a symbol that traces straight back to the runic language of the Jelling Stones — a striking testament to how far these [runes](/en/blog/elder-futhark-runes) have travelled.

## 7. UNESCO World Heritage: Why Jelling Remains Denmark's Cradle

Since 1994, the Jelling Stones, the burial mounds, and the church have together held UNESCO World Heritage status, with the organization explicitly noting that they provide "an outstanding record of Viking culture in the 10th century." For Denmark, the site carries an even deeper symbolic weight: the Danish royal family, whose lineage can formally be traced back to Gorm the Old, occasionally calls Jelling itself the "cradle of the Danish monarchy." Standing between the stones, beneath the same mounds where a king once rested in pagan earth, you can watch the exact moment a kingdom reinvented itself, carved in stone, written in runes, and still legible today to anyone willing to look closely. A modern visitor center at the foot of the mounds now walks through, layer by layer, everything two centuries of excavation have uncovered there, making Jelling one of the rare places where the Viking Age can be not just read about, but literally walked through.
