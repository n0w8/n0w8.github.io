Three triangles, locked into each other like a puzzle with no visible start or end — few symbols from the Viking Age look as enigmatic today as the **Valknut**. You'll find it carved into memorial stones next to dying warriors, worked into grave goods, tattooed on forearms in any city you care to name. But ask the simple question of what the **Valknut symbol** actually **means**, and the room goes quiet fast. There's no runic inscription that explains it. No line in the Eddas that even names it. Just stones, a fragment of woven cloth, and a century of scholarly guesswork.

That, honestly, is what makes it worth writing about. If you're after a tidy, confirmed answer, you won't get one here. If you want to see how historians build a credible case out of scraps of evidence, keep reading.

## Where the Name Actually Comes From

Start with the obvious. The word **Valknut** is not a Viking Age term at all — it's a modern Norwegian and Swedish compound built from *valr* ("those slain in battle") and *knut* ("knot"), giving us the **"knot of the slain."** Folklorists most likely coined this label in the 19th century, once they started cataloguing old symbols systematically.

Which means we don't actually know what the Vikings called the sign themselves. Maybe nothing more specific than "the knot" or "the mark." That gap sets the tone for everything that follows: the **Valknut** is an archaeological object with no surviving caption, and every meaning attached to it since is a scholarly reconstruction, not a recorded fact.

## The Gotland Picture Stones: Where the Valknut Actually Appears

The strongest physical evidence for the **Valknut** comes from the Swedish island of **Gotland**, specifically from two well-known picture stones. The first, **Stora Hammars I**, stands in the parish of Lärbro and shows, across several stacked picture fields, a whole sequence of scenes: combat, a man bent face-down over what some researchers read as an altar (a possible sacrifice scene), a rider with a raised spear — and, unmistakably, right in the middle of it all, the **Valknut**. Next to it sits a bird some scholars identify as a raven, an animal long associated with Odin.

The second stone, **Tängelgårda**, also on Gotland, shows a procession of armed riders and, once again, the pattern of three interlocked triangles — this time near a figure some archaeologists read as a fallen warrior being received by a female figure. Female figures in scenes like this are traditionally linked to the **valkyries**, the warrior-women who, as we cover in more depth in our article on the [valkyries](/en/blog/valkyries), were said to guide the slain to Valhalla.

Both stones are generally dated to the 7th or 8th century by scholars — meaning they sit right at the tail end of the Vendel Period, bleeding into the early Viking Age. What matters most here is context: the **Valknut** never shows up in isolation on these stones. It's always embedded in scenes of death, combat, and — by the most common reading — the journey into the afterlife.

## The Third Find: The Oseberg Tapestry

A third important piece of evidence isn't stone at all — it's cloth. The famous **Oseberg ship burial** in Norway, excavated in 1904 and dated to roughly 834 CE, preserved an extraordinary set of grave goods, including the ornate longship itself, which we cover in detail in our piece on the [Viking longship](/en/blog/the-longship). Among the burial's contents were fragments of a woven tapestry. On one badly weathered fragment, a procession of carts and figures is still visible, and at one point in the design the same three-triangle knot pattern reappears — identified by many specialists as a **Valknut**.

The burial context is worth sitting with. A ship burial was one of the most elaborate funerary rites of the Viking Age, reserved for people of real status. That the same symbol found on Gotland's memorial stones turns up here too, in a burial built entirely around death, transition, and the honoring of the dead, strengthens the case that the **Valknut** genuinely had something to do with dying and the passage beyond. It doesn't prove it outright, but it's a striking pattern.

## Three Triangles, Two Different Knots

Here's something most people miss: "the" **Valknut** isn't actually one fixed geometric shape. At least two distinct constructions circulate under the same name. The version documented on the Gotland stones is built from three triangles overlapping in a way that forms a single continuous, "unicursal" weave — meaning you could, in theory, draw the entire figure without ever lifting the pen. That construction fits neatly alongside other unicursal knotwork common in Viking Age picture-stone art and woodcarving: interlaced beasts, endless bands biting their own tails.

The second version, far more common in modern jewelry, follows the logic of a Borromean ring instead: three separate, closed triangles arranged so that no single one holds the other two together on its own — remove any one of them, and the whole thing falls apart. Medieval carvers clearly understood this kind of interlacing; similar patterns turn up in both Celtic and Norse ornamental traditions. But which version the original stone carvers actually intended is hard to pin down given centuries of weathering. For the symbol's modern popularity, the distinction barely registers. For archaeological precision, though, it matters a great deal — it's a reminder that a good deal of what we now picture as "the" Valknut has been standardized by modern redrawings, not preserved unchanged from the original carvings.

## What Scholars Think It Might Mean

Here's where it gets genuinely interesting, and where it's important to keep fact and hypothesis clearly separated. Research has mostly settled into two overlapping interpretations.

The first ties the **Valknut** to **Odin** himself. Old Norse sources repeatedly connect the All-Father to a concept called *herfjöturr*, the **"war-fetter"** — a sudden battlefield paralysis Odin was said to inflict on warriors, a kind of mental block that could turn courage into panic or freeze a fighter mid-strike. Some scholars, including the historian **Hilda Ellis Davidson**, have suggested that the tangled, seemingly unsolvable triangles of the **Valknut** could be a visual expression of exactly this idea — a "mind-knot" nobody can untie. Under this reading, the symbol works almost like Odin's personal seal over life and death in combat.

The second interpretation leans harder into the cult of the dead. Because the **Valknut** consistently turns up near combat, death, and probable valkyrie imagery on both the Gotland stones and the Oseberg fragment, it's reasonable to suspect it functioned as a **mark of an honorable death in battle** — something like a visual ticket to Valhalla, granted to those who fell fighting. The two readings aren't mutually exclusive, either: a symbol marking Odin's control over a warrior's fate in battle could just as easily double as a sign of the honorable death he grants the fallen.

> Nearly every serious researcher adds the same caveat here: no surviving medieval text — not the Poetic Edda, not the Prose Edda, no saga, no runic inscription — names this symbol or explains what it did. Everything we "know" is inferred from the images surrounding it.

## Why We Don't Know the Exact Meaning

It's worth pausing on that point rather than rushing past it. We're conditioned to expect old symbols to carry one fixed, settled meaning, the way we expect a modern logo to. But the Viking Age left us no manual for its visual language. Snorri Sturluson, the Icelander we owe much of our knowledge of the [Norse gods](/en/blog/norse-gods) to, didn't compile the Prose Edda until around 1220 — roughly four hundred years after the Gotland stones were carved, and in a country that had been Christian for generations by then. He never once mentions the **Valknut**.

None of that makes the interpretations above wrong. The repeated pairing with battle and death, the thematic proximity to Odin, and the symbol's consistent appearance on objects of high ritual weight — a ship burial, memorial stones — make the Odin-and-death hypothesis by far the most plausible reading available. But plausible is not the same as proven. Serious runic scholarship, including work coming out of Uppsala University, deliberately talks in terms of "likely associations" rather than settled facts. Some researchers also point out that similar knotwork patterns show up in purely decorative Viking Age art with no ritual context attached at all — a reminder that not every instance of the pattern was necessarily loaded with religious meaning.

## Related Symbols, and a Necessary Distinction

The **Valknut** occasionally gets mixed up with other triangular or interlaced motifs, such as the so-called triquetra-style "triple horn" patterns or later Celtic knotwork. That's a modern conflation, not a historical connection. Popular writing sometimes also links it to **Yggdrasil**, the Norse [world tree](/en/blog/yggdrasil-world-tree) — nine roots, nine worlds, three sets of three corners — but there's no source evidence for that connection whatsoever. It's an appealing bit of modern numerology, not documented Viking Age symbolism.

This is exactly where a vague archaeological trace turns into an "ancient truth" online. Taking the **Valknut** seriously means respecting that boundary. We know the contexts in which the symbol appears. We know which interpretations are plausible. We do not know the words an eighth-century Norseman would have used to explain the sign himself.

## An Old Riddle's Modern Life

Since the 1990s, the **Valknut** has taken on a second life as jewelry, tattoo art, and an identifying mark within various strands of modern heathenry (Ásatrú) — and, unfortunately, within extremist subcultures that have appropriated Norse imagery for their own ends. That's a separate, important story on its own, and it doesn't change a single fact about the Viking Age archaeological record. Anyone engaging with the symbol seriously should keep the historical evidence and its modern, often politically charged reuse firmly apart.

What's left is a symbol that fascinates precisely because it resists a final answer. Three triangles, inseparably woven together, found at the edges of death — on memorial stones for the fallen, inside a ship burial, apparently connected to Odin and those who deliver the dead to him. That's as far as honesty lets us go. But maybe that's the right quality for a sign so tightly bound to the biggest riddle of all: death itself.
