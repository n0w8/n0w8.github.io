Did they really exist — women who stood in the shield wall with sword and shield, fighting alongside men? **Shield-maidens** are one of the most stubborn open questions in Viking studies, and their sheer popularity has made the answer harder to pin down, not easier. Television has turned them into unquestioned heroines. The actual evidence is far messier: a tangle of sagas, one heavily debated grave, and no shortage of wishful thinking on every side of the argument.

The honest answer is that we don't know for certain — and that uncertainty is exactly what makes this worth digging into properly. Let's look at what the sources actually say.

## What "shield-maiden" actually means

First, a distinction that gets lost too often. A **shield-maiden** (Old Norse *skjaldmær*) is a mortal woman who fights as a warrior — not a supernatural being. That sets her apart from the **Valkyries**, Odin's fate-spirits who choose who dies on the battlefield without ever swinging a weapon themselves. If you want the mythological side of that story, our piece on the [Valkyries](/en/blog/valkyries) covers it in full — that article is about myth, this one is about a historical and literary figure of flesh and blood, or at least the claim of one.

The line between the two blurs constantly in the sources themselves, which only adds to the confusion. Some poets use "shield-maiden" almost interchangeably with "valkyrie," while others clearly mean a flesh-and-blood fighter. That blurriness is itself the first clue to how complicated this subject gets.

## Saxo Grammaticus and the 300 shield-maidens of Bråvalla

The most detailed — and most problematic — source on shield-maidens doesn't come from Scandinavia at all, but from a Danish clergyman. **Saxo Grammaticus** wrote his **Gesta Danorum** around 1200, a Latin history of the Danes that cheerfully blends mythic prehistory with semi-historical events. Saxo describes the legendary **Battle of Bråvalla**, in which some 300 shield-maidens supposedly fought for the Swedish king Sigurd Ring, led by a woman named **Webiorg** and the more famous **Hetha**.

Saxo himself was visibly uneasy about the idea. He writes, in essence, that such women had "unsexed" themselves, trading femininity for warlike hardness — a line that reads far more like moral commentary than reportage. That's telling: Saxo doesn't recount shield-maidens because he admires them, but because he sees them as a symptom of a chaotic, pre-Christian world gone slightly off its hinges.

The same author also gives us the earliest surviving version of the **Lagertha** legend — the warrior who allegedly fought beside Ragnar Lothbrok and later married him. Saxo describes her fighting with her hair loose and streaming over her shoulders, an image that has survived into essentially every modern retelling since. The catch: Saxo was writing roughly 400 years after Ragnar supposedly lived, working within a literary tradition that dressed up hero legends for effect. As eyewitness testimony, it simply doesn't hold up.

## The Hervarar saga: Hervör, the grave-robber

A second key text is the **Hervarar saga ok Heiðreks**, one of the Icelandic Fornaldarsögur ("sagas of ancient times"), written down in the 13th century. Its heroine, **Hervör**, is one of the most striking warrior-women in all of Old Norse literature.

Hervör grows up as the daughter of a slain warrior, dresses as a man, calls herself "Hervarðr" for a time, and eventually sails to the burial mound of her dead father **Angantýr** to reclaim his cursed sword, **Tyrfing**. The saga renders the confrontation with real force:

> Hervör calls into the burial fires: "Do not wake me with your magic if you will not give me the sword the dwarves forged. It is unseemly for the dead to hoard weapons in the grave while I, a woman, stand before you unafraid."

Hervör gets the sword and spends years afterward roaming as a Viking and a fighter, before eventually settling down to marry and raise a family. That last detail matters more than it might seem: the saga treats her warrior years not as a permanent identity but as a phase of life she later trades for marriage and motherhood. That pattern recurs across several sagas — unmarried young women are occasionally granted license to take on male roles, before the social order reasserts itself and reclaims them.

## What the archaeology actually shows: Birka Bj 581

For decades, skeptics could lean on one reliable argument: sagas are literature, not history, and not a single grave proved an armed Viking woman ever existed. That argument collapsed in 2017.

Grave **Bj 581** in **Birka**, Sweden's most important Viking-Age trading center, had originally been excavated back in 1878 by the archaeologist Hjalmar Stolpe. It held an impressive warrior's kit: a sword, a battle-axe, a spear, arrows, two shields, and — notably — a complete gaming set with pieces, an object often linked to tactical thinking in war. For well over a century, the grave was routinely described, in essentially every museum catalog, as belonging to a male warrior.

In 2017, a team led by osteologist **Anna Kjellström** and geneticist **Charlotte Hedenstierna-Jonson** published a DNA analysis of the skeleton in the *American Journal of Physical Anthropology*. The result: no Y chromosome, but two X chromosomes. The individual in Bj 581 was biologically female.

The debate that followed hasn't really ended. Critics raised a legitimate methodological concern: given 19th-century excavation methods, the bones in the grave could have been mixed with remains from a neighboring burial. A 2019 follow-up study led by Hedenstierna-Jonson pushed back on that, finding no evidence of commingling — every skeletal element examined belonged to a single individual. Other researchers, including archaeologist Neil Price, backed the warrior-woman interpretation; others, like Judith Jesch, urged caution, pointing out that weapons in a grave don't prove the woman herself ever fought — they could equally reflect status, inheritance, or a command role that never involved swinging a sword personally.

That's the actual state of the scholarly argument today, and honesty requires leaving it exactly there: **Bj 581 proves that a biological woman was buried with a complete warrior's equipment. It does not, by itself, prove that she personally fought in battle.**

## More graves, more open questions

Bj 581 isn't entirely alone, even if it's the best-known case. A grave at **Solør**, Norway, excavated back in the 19th century and containing a sword, axe, spear, and shield, was long attributed to a man — only a 2019 osteological re-examination identified the remains as female. Scattered finds from **Nordre Kjølen** in Norway and a handful of Danish sites also show women buried with weapons, though in far smaller numbers than the corresponding male burials.

These finds remain the exception. The overwhelming majority of Viking-Age weapon graves belong to men, and the overwhelming majority of women's graves contain typically female grave goods — jewelry, weaving weights, keys — objects tied to running a household, itself a genuinely powerful role in Viking society. Drawing a whole class of regular female warriors from a handful of graves stretches the evidence too far. Dismissing those graves entirely does the same thing in the opposite direction.

## Rus chronicles and Byzantine testimony

One more often-overlooked clue comes from an entirely different direction: accounts of the **Rus**, the Swedish-descended Vikings who ran trade routes down to Constantinople and across what is now Russia. The Byzantine chronicler **John Skylitzes**, describing the fighting between the Rus and the Byzantine army in 971 at the siege of **Dorostolon**, notes that among the Rus dead the Byzantines found on the battlefield afterward were armed women.

The note is brief, offhand, and comes from a foreign hand rather than a Norse storyteller — which in some ways makes it more credible than an embellished saga, since a Byzantine chronicler had no obvious motive to invent such a detail. It doesn't prove an institutionalized caste of women warriors, but it fits a pattern: isolated, real cases of armed women within a fundamentally male-dominated warrior culture.

## Why the question stays so contentious

The shield-maiden debate runs hot largely because it's rarely conducted as pure history. On one side sits a legitimate desire to correct a Viking narrative that has often flattened women out of the picture, and to restore their real role in that society — as traders, estate-holders, priestesses, and, perhaps, as fighters too. On the other side sits an equally legitimate worry about reading a modern wish back into evidence that's simply too thin to support it.

Both extremes overreach. Claiming shield-maidens fought routinely in organized formations alongside men stretches the evidence well past its breaking point. But claiming no woman *ever* took up arms ignores Bj 581, Solør, and Skylitzes' testimony. The most honest position sits in between: individual women probably did fight, or at least were buried with full warrior's equipment — but there's no evidence of regular, socially institutionalized units of women, of the kind Saxo's 300 shield-maidens at Bråvalla describe. The mass phenomenon remains legend. The individual case is plausible.

It's also worth considering how such women would have been armed. The Vikings' iconic longship carried warriors of both sexes across enormous distances — readers curious about the technology behind those voyages can find more in our article on the [Viking longship](/en/blog/the-longship). And a woman who genuinely fought as a shield-maiden almost certainly used the same weapons as her male contemporaries: a round shield, a spear, an axe, and, more rarely, the expensive sword — the very kind found in Bj 581.

## Fiction, exaggeration, and the hard kernel of truth

What's left, in the end, is an observation that fits Viking studies as a whole: fiction got there first, and archaeology only catches up centuries later — sometimes confirming the story, sometimes undercutting it, usually doing a bit of both. Saxo Grammaticus and the author of the Hervarar saga told stories about shield-maidens because such figures make for compelling narrative: transgression, vengeance, honor. Whether they were describing a genuine, if uncommon, social reality, or inflating a rare exception into legend, isn't something the text alone can settle.

Bj 581 shifts the debate without closing it. It shows that at least one Viking-Age woman was buried with the full equipment of a warrior, in one of Scandinavia's most important trading hubs. That's not enough to confirm Saxo's 300 fighters at Bråvalla. But it's also not nothing — not enough, either, to write off Hervör and her literary sisters as pure invention.

Maybe that's the right stance toward the Viking Age generally: neither swallowing the romantic image whole, nor discarding it out of sheer caution. The shield-maiden was probably rare — but despite every reasonable doubt, she probably wasn't just a story someone made up.
